A feminist of today as a reflection of the past

Feminism has always adapted to its time. It is the same for its protagonists.

Therefore, the dominant figures of the movement have a multitude of different profiles.

And sometimes; in spite of the convergences of ideas between the various waves of feminism, and a gap of time that can go until several centuries.

It happens; in the history of feminism, to perceive a strong symbolic relation between individuals. That it passes by their courses of engagement in the struggle or their more personal adventures. Despite the geographical or temporal distance. They are naturally affiliated to each other by their past experiences. The case of Ada Lovelace and Margaret Hamilton is one such case.

Feminist and mathematics queen

Born on December 10, 1815, to two wealthy and cultured parents. The young Londoner Ada Lovelace discovered her passion for mathematics very early on. A passion that her mother would encourage and help to maintain, despite the mores of the time, preventing young girls from receiving an education as advanced as their male counterparts.

Following her meeting with the mathematician Charles Babbage, Ada became fascinated by his work on his calculating machines. (We are talking here about the ancestor of computer science as we imagine it today).

(Galery)

After a complicated pursuit of mathematics due to a fragile health. Ada decided in 1843 to devote all his work to Babbage's analytical machine.

Following a series of frenetic works on the Babbage machine, Ada will compensate an algorithm whose goal was to calculate a sequence of rational numbers (the Bernoulli numbers). The resulting program will be considered as the very first computer program in the world.

Thanks to her passion for mathematics, Ada Lovelace demonstrated to the world that when given the same opportunities as men. Women are also capable of excelling in intellectual fields.

Although she never spoke out on the subject. Her story brought recognition to all the female figures hidden by the shadow of men, and encouraged every woman to follow what they are passionate about even in fields that close the doors to them, making her a key figure of feminism, especially when it meets the world of science.

The woman who landed the man on the moon

On Monday, July 21, 1969, more than a century after the major advances of Ada. Humanity set foot on the moon.

A titanic feat that would probably have remained in fiction for a long time, without the help of one person.

That of Margaret Hamilton, the woman in charge of developing the software for the Apollo 11 mission.

Since her teenage years, Margaret was infatuated with the same passion as her predecessor and even went so far as to obtain her degree in mathematics, giving her the right to teach this discipline.

By refusing to become a housewife and devote her life to her husband, Margaret freed herself from the role that American society reserved for women, and took on the role of a true feminist pioneer.

In 1960 she accepted a position at MIT and was in charge of developing meteorological software. A year later she joined the SAGE Project, one of the first computerized missile defense systems, and from the very first days she shone with her extraordinary skills, successfully solving a problem that had not been solved until then. It was this same feat that opened the doors to NASA in 1963.

His new responsibilities were to design the Apollo program's onboard system, making it as autonomous and intelligent as possible.

The least we can say is that Hamilton is fulfilled by this new position. So much so that during her long programming sessions, she often decided to bring her daughter to the office to keep her company and present her programs.

It is still considered today that Margaret Hamilton laid the foundations of modern computing. NASA will reward her in 2003 and President Obama in 2017 for her contribution to the discovery of space and computing.

A timeless lesson in feminism

The similarity between Ada Lovelace and Margaret Hamilton teaches us that man has always been wrong to deprive himself of the intelligence of women in intellectual fields.

That when a person is driven by passion and the will to advance humanity, her origins had no place in the debate.

Their stories have feminist lessons that we must learn: no matter what the times and environments, behind every progress of Man there are women.
